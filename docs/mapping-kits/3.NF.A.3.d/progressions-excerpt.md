# Progressions excerpt — 3.NF.A.3.d

**Source:** Progressions-3-5-Fractions.pdf, §Grade 3 — "Comparing fractions," extracted page 10.

## Verbatim excerpt

> Comparing fractions. Previously, in Grade 2, students compared lengths using a standard unit of measurement. 2.MD.3 In Grade 3, they build on this idea to compare fractions with the same denominator. They see that for two fractions that have the same denominator, the underlying unit fractions are the same size, so the fraction with the greater numerator is greater because it is made of more unit fractions. For example, on the number line the segment from 0 to 3/4 is shorter than the segment from 0 to 5/4 because it is 3 fourths long as opposed to 5 fourths long. Therefore 3/4 < 5/4. 3.NF.3d (p. 10)

> In Grade 2, students gained experience with the inverse relationship between the size of a physical length-unit and the number of length-units required to cover a given distance. 2.MD.2 Grade 3 students see that for unit fractions, the one with the larger denominator is smaller, by reasoning, for example, that in order for more (identical) pieces to make the same whole, the pieces must be smaller. From this they reason that for two fractions that have the same numerator, the fraction with the smaller denominator is greater. For example, 2/5 > 2/7, because 1/7 < 1/5, so 2 lengths of 1/7 is less than 2 lengths of 1/5. (p. 10)

> As with equivalence of fractions, it is important in comparing fractions to make sure that each fraction refers to the same whole. (p. 10)

> A student might think that 1/4 > 1/2, because a fourth of the pizza on the right is bigger than a half of the pizza on the left. (p. 10, caption)

> As students move towards understanding fractions as points on the number line, they develop an understanding of order in terms of position. Given two fractions — thus two points on the number line — the one to the left is said to be smaller and the one to right is said to be larger. This understanding of order as position will become important in Grade 6 when students start working with negative numbers. (p. 10)

Sidebar — the standard verbatim:

> 3.NF.3 Explain equivalence of fractions in special cases, and compare fractions by reasoning about their size.
>
> d. Compare two fractions with the same numerator or the same denominator by reasoning about their size. Recognize that comparisons are valid only when the two fractions refer to the same whole. Record the results of comparisons with the symbols >, =, or <, and justify the conclusions, e.g., by using a visual fraction model. (p. 10)

## Extraction for drafters

**What the kid should be able to do**

- Compare two fractions with the **same denominator** by comparing numerators (more unit fractions ⇒ larger).
- Compare two fractions with the **same numerator** by reasoning about denominators (smaller denominator ⇒ larger unit fraction ⇒ larger fraction).
- Record comparisons with >, =, <.
- Justify the comparison with a visual fraction model (number line, tape, area).
- Recognize that comparisons are only valid when both fractions refer to the same whole.

**Common misconceptions / developmental hurdles named in the Progressions**

- **"Bigger denominator means bigger fraction"** — students transfer whole-number intuition (5 > 2, so 1/5 > 1/2) incorrectly. The Progressions counters: more identical pieces to make the same whole means smaller pieces (p. 10).
- **Same-whole error** — comparing two fractions whose wholes differ: "a fourth of the pizza on the right is bigger than a half of the pizza on the left" (p. 10). The comparison 1/4 > 1/2 is invalid because the pizzas are different sizes.
- The Progressions flags "order as position" on the number line — left = smaller, right = larger — as the bridge to Grade 6 work with negatives.

**Where this skill sits in the progression**

- Earlier: 3.NF.A.1 (a/b as a copies of 1/b), 3.NF.A.2 (fractions on the number line), 2.MD.3 (compare lengths with a standard unit), 2.MD.2 (inverse size-unit / number-of-units relationship).
- Later: 4.NF.A.2 (compare fractions with different numerators AND different denominators — using common denominators, common numerators, or benchmarks like 1/2).

**Recommended representations / models named in the Progressions**

- Number line diagrams (segment-length comparison: 0-to-3/4 vs 0-to-5/4).
- Visual fraction models (the standard explicitly names them).
- Same-pizza / same-whole drawings to make the "same whole" requirement concrete.
